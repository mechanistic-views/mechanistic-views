# Gauge Quotients and Holonomy

This is the full mathematical treatment of the [fiber bundle quotient formalism](/formalism/fiber-bundle-quotient/), which underpins the [structural view](/views/structural/). For the accessible overview, start with the [parent page](/formalism/fiber-bundle-quotient/).

Transformer parameter space has symmetries. Two weight configurations related by certain transformations implement the same input-output function. These **gauge transformations** define equivalence classes. Any mechanism claim that depends on a coordinate inside an equivalence class is a potential parameterization artifact.

Holonomy is a gauge-invariant quantity that can serve as a mechanism fingerprint. The construction here is formally analogous to holonomy in gauge theory (Yang-Mills, electromagnetism). The analogy is productive but limited: in physics, holonomy has physical significance grounded in the Ambrose-Singer theorem relating holonomy to field strength, and the connection is the dynamical variable with equations of motion from a Lagrangian. In transformers, the gauge redundancy arises from the parameterization of the function, not from any dynamical principle, and the connection is chosen by convention rather than derived. Claims about holonomy-based mechanism identity should be understood within this more limited formal analogy.

## Gauge symmetries in transformers

The following symmetries hold exactly for transformers without LayerNorm, embedding weight tying, or tied positional encodings.

**Permutation symmetry.** Swapping $h_1$ and $h_2$ and simultaneously permuting corresponding rows of $W^Q, W^K, W^V, W^O$ preserves function exactly. This symmetry survives LayerNorm.

**Orthogonal rotation symmetry.** In a transformer without LayerNorm or biases, rotating the residual stream at a given position by $U \in O(d)$ and rotating all weight matrices reading from or writing to that position preserves function exactly. LayerNorm breaks this: it normalizes the residual stream, so the rotation and the weight rotation do not compose to the identity through the normalization. This symmetry holds exactly only without LayerNorm; approximately when the LayerNorm scale is large.

**Rescaling symmetry.** Query-key scores are invariant to simultaneous rescaling of $W^Q$ and $W^K$ by inverse factors. Survives LayerNorm.

The **gauge group** $\mathcal{G}$ is generated by these transformations. For real transformers with LayerNorm, $\mathcal{G}$ is primarily exact head permutations plus approximate rotations.

## Freeness and the generic position requirement

The principal fiber bundle construction requires $\mathcal{G}$ to act **freely** on $\mathcal{W}$: no non-identity transformation fixes any weight configuration. For permutation symmetry, this fails when two heads at the same layer have identical weights — permuting them fixes the configuration. At such **fixed points**, the quotient $\mathcal{W}/\mathcal{G}$ is locally an orbifold, not a manifold, and the fiber bundle structure breaks down. The holonomy group is not defined at fixed points.

Trained models are generically not at fixed points, but models with similar heads (common after convergence) are approximately at fixed points. The bundle and holonomy constructions are accurate for generic models with distinct heads; they are approximations otherwise. All claims involving holonomy implicitly assume generic position.

## The gauge quotient

The **gauge orbit** of $\theta$ is $[\theta] = \{g \cdot \theta : g \in \mathcal{G}\}$. The **gauge quotient** $\mathcal{W}/\mathcal{G}$ is the space of functionally distinct models. A mechanism claim is **gauge-invariant** if it depends only on $[\theta]$.

**Gauge-invariant quantities**: singular values of $W^{OV}$, principal angles between subspaces, effective rank. Composition scores $\|W^{OV}_u \cdot W^{KQ}_v\|_F$ are invariant under head permutations but *not* under the orthogonal rotation symmetry. Under a residual-stream rotation $U$, all weight matrices reading from or writing to that position rotate simultaneously: $W^{OV} \to W^{OV}$ (invariant, as noted above) but $W^{KQ} = (W^K)^\top W^Q \to U W^{KQ}$, so $W^{OV}_u \cdot W^{KQ}_v \to W^{OV}_u \cdot U W^{KQ}_v \neq W^{OV}_u \cdot W^{KQ}_v$ in general. Composition scores are gauge-invariant only with respect to the permutation subgroup of $\mathcal{G}$.

**Gauge-covariant quantities**: specific head indices, individual neuron activations, basis-dependent direction vectors.

## Fiber bundle structure

At generic points, $\mathcal{W} \to \mathcal{W}/\mathcal{G}$ is a **principal fiber bundle** with structure group $\mathcal{G}$.

The bundle carries a **connection** — a horizontal subspace at each point specifying how nearby fibers are identified. **The choice of connection is not canonical.** Two natural choices:

- Orthogonal complement to the fiber under the **Euclidean metric** on $\mathcal{W}$
- Orthogonal complement under the **Fisher information metric** on $\mathcal{W}$

These give different parallel transports and therefore different holonomy groups. In physics, the connection is the dynamical variable with equations of motion; here, there is no such principle, and the connection is a free parameter in the theory. Claims about holonomy should specify which connection is used. The default in this site is the Euclidean metric connection.

The connection enables **parallel transport**: a subspace $S$ at $\theta_0$ can be transported along a path in $\mathcal{W}/\mathcal{G}$ to produce a subspace at $\theta_1$.

## Holonomy

Parallel-transporting a subspace $S$ around a *closed* loop may return a rotated subspace $S' = SR$ for some $R \in O(k)$. The **holonomy** of the loop is $R$. The **holonomy group** $\mathrm{Hol}(S)$ is the set of all such transformations.

Properties:

1. **Gauge invariance.** $\mathrm{Hol}(S)$ depends only on $[S]$, not on any representative. Proof: holonomy is defined on the base $\mathcal{W}/\mathcal{G}$, not in fiber coordinates.

2. **Curvature.** Trivial holonomy ($\mathrm{Hol}(S) = \{I\}$) iff the connection is flat near $S$. The Ambrose-Singer theorem (in the exact gauge theory analogy) equates the holonomy Lie algebra with the curvature 2-form; the formal analogue holds here, though the physical significance is less direct.

3. **Connection dependence.** Different connections give different holonomy groups for the same mechanism. This is a real free parameter.

## The holonomy identity conjecture

**Conjecture.**

*Necessary direction* (established): same mechanism (same gauge orbit) implies isomorphic holonomy groups. Follows from gauge-invariance.

*Sufficient direction* (open): isomorphic holonomy groups imply the same mechanism. Not established. Two mechanisms could have isomorphic holonomy groups by coincidence of symmetry structure — analogously to non-isomorphic groups having isomorphic abelianizations. Sufficiency would require additional conditions (same connected component of mechanism space; holonomy representation together with base point determines gauge orbit) that are not verified for natural transformer mechanisms (OQ 5.4).

The conjecture is used in its established necessary-direction form: *isomorphic holonomy is a necessary condition for cross-model mechanism identity*.

## Computational tractability

**[Partial — see below]** Different components of the gauge formalism vary widely in tractability.

- **Gauge-invariant quantities** (singular values of $W^{OV}$, principal angles, effective rank): tractable, $O(d^3)$ per head, feasible at scale.
- **Composition scores** $\|W^{OV}_u \cdot W^{KQ}_v\|_F$: tractable, $O(d^2 h^2)$ for all pairs in a layer.
- **Holonomy estimation**: requires numerical parallel transport along a path in weight space. Feasible for short paths (few checkpoints), but the path and connection must be specified. Not yet standardized as a practical tool.
- **Holonomy group identification**: requires inferring group structure from sample holonomy elements — an algebraic problem that is unsolved in general. **[Open — practically intractable at scale]**

## Empirical estimation

1. Start with trained model $M_0$, causal subspace $S_0$ at layer $\ell$
2. Construct a closed path in $\mathcal{W}/\mathcal{G}$ through $[M_0]$ — e.g., linearly interpolate in weight space between $M_0$ and some $M'$, then back, **applying gauge normalization at each step** (e.g., canonical head-permutation alignment) to ensure the path stays in a consistent gauge orbit representative. Without gauge normalization, the path is in $\mathcal{W}$, not $\mathcal{W}/\mathcal{G}$, and the accumulated transformation reflects gauge drift, not holonomy.
3. At each step, parallel-transport $S$ along the path using the chosen connection: find the subspace minimizing geodesic distance to the linearized-transported subspace
4. The transformation $R$ with $S_0 R \approx S_n$ estimates the holonomy of that loop

Not applied to transformer circuits at scale (OQ 5.2).

## Stability and the connection-dependence caveat

High holonomy curvature indicates geometric instability under fine-tuning. Stability predictions depend on the connection choice and should specify it.
