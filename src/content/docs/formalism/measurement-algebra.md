# Measurement Algebra

The formalism for the [perspectival view](/views/perspectival/).

## What it is

Measurement algebra refers to the algebraic structure of measurement operations and their compositions. When multiple measurement methods are applied to the same system, the results can be composed, compared, and checked for consistency using algebraic operations — products, projections, commutators.

The core idea is that measurements are not passive observations but active operations that can interact, interfere, and compose. The algebra of these operations captures what can and cannot be jointly measured.

See [Measurement in quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement_in_quantum_mechanics) and [Operator algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_algebra) on Wikipedia for the physics origins. The application here is structural, not quantum-mechanical.

## How the perspectival view uses it

Under the perspectival view, a mechanism is a projection of the method used to find it — different methods may reveal different aspects of the same underlying structure. Measurement algebra provides the formalism because the central question is how different method-projections relate to each other.

Two perspectival descriptions refer to the same mechanism when they exhibit *cross-method coherence*: the measurements from different methods compose consistently under the algebra. If method A and method B produce results that are algebraically compatible — commuting projections, consistent composition — they are measuring the same thing from different angles.

The perspectival view does not claim that any single method reveals the full mechanism. The algebra of measurements is the right tool because it formalizes what "consistent across methods" means without privileging any single method's output.
