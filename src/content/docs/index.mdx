# Mechanistic Views

*A five-axis ontology for mechanistic interpretability*

Neural networks are remarkably capable, but the internal mechanisms that produce these capabilities are still only partly understood. Mechanistic interpretability investigates this, and the field has identified circuits, features, causal subspaces, formation dynamics, and more, across a range of models and tasks. Individual components have been assigned functional roles like "name movers" and "induction heads." But what exactly is a "name mover"? Is it a specific component (head 9.9 in layer 9 of this particular model)? A functional role that any component could play, in any model? A direction in the model's internal representation space? A structural pattern in the weight matrices?

These are not the same claim. Each assumes something different about what a mechanism is, what counts as evidence, and what follows from it. The same result means different things under different assumptions. Without explicit answers to these questions, a mechanistic claim is incomplete.

We call these background assumptions a **mechanistic view**.

## What is a mechanistic view?

A mechanistic view is the set of commitments — explicit or implicit — underlying a mechanistic claim. It is formalized as five questions:

| Axis | Question |
|---|---|
| **Ontology** | What kind of entity counts as a mechanism? |
| **Identity** | When do two descriptions refer to the same mechanism? |
| **Evidence** | What measurements can warrant a claim about a mechanism? |
| **Formalism** | What mathematical language expresses the claim? |
| **Target** | What phenomenon is the mechanism supposed to explain? |

![Five axes of a mechanistic view: Ontology, Identity, Evidence, Formalism, Target](/mechanistic-views/figures/five-axes-pipeline-clean.svg)

The five answers are not independent — what a mechanism is constrains when two are the same, which constrains what formalism is needed. See [Framework](/mechanistic-views/framework/) for the formal definition, coherence conditions, and why these constraints matter.

## Eight mechanistic views

The five axes admit multiple coherent answers. We organize these into eight mechanistic views, ordered by increasing ontological commitment — how much they claim about what mechanisms are, independent of any measurement:

$$\text{Instrumental} < \text{Perspectival} < \text{Object} < \text{Role} < \text{Subspace} < \text{Structural} < \text{Process} < \text{Stratified}$$

Higher-commitment views make stronger claims but require more evidence. We propose the structural, process, and stratified views as novel research programs.

| View | Ontology | Identity | Evidence | Formalism | Target |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| [Instrumental](/mechanistic-views/views/instrumental/) | Predictive model | Predictive equivalence | [Forecast, intervention utility](/mechanistic-views/views/instrumental/#evidence) | [Model theory](/mechanistic-views/formalism/model-theory/) | Behavioral prediction |
| [Perspectival](/mechanistic-views/views/perspectival/) | Method projection | Cross-method coherence | [Multi-method robustness](/mechanistic-views/views/perspectival/#evidence) | [Measurement algebra](/mechanistic-views/formalism/measurement-algebra/) | Method-relative |
| [Object](/mechanistic-views/views/object/) | Concrete part | Component overlap | [Ablation, patching](/mechanistic-views/views/object/#evidence) | [Directed graph](/mechanistic-views/formalism/directed-graph/) | Specific behavior |
| [Role](/mechanistic-views/views/role/) | Functional role | Role equivalence | [Role-specific causal tests](/mechanistic-views/views/role/#evidence) | [Role graph](/mechanistic-views/formalism/role-graph/) | Functional class |
| [Subspace](/mechanistic-views/views/subspace/) | Causal subspace | Same projector; $d_{\mathrm{Gr}} < \theta$ | [DAS/IIA (linear)](/mechanistic-views/views/subspace/#evidence), [subspace stability](/mechanistic-views/formalism/grassmannian/#subspace-stability) | [Grassmannian $\mathrm{Gr}(k,d)$](/mechanistic-views/formalism/grassmannian/) | Representational variable |

## Relationship to Mechanistic Validity

Mechanistic Views defines what a mechanism is — what kind of object you're claiming exists, when two are the same, what formalism expresses the claim. [*Mechanistic Validity*](https://mechanistic-validity.github.io/mechanistic-validity/) is the evaluation counterpart: a six-layer pipeline from description modes to evidence families, calibrated metrics, falsifiable criteria, validity types, and tiered verdicts. Which validity types apply and what counts as passing them depends on the view — a subspace claim requires different evidence than a component claim.

Start with **[Framework](/mechanistic-views/framework/)** for the formal definition and coherence conditions. Then read one or two view pages — **[Object](/mechanistic-views/views/object/)** and **[Subspace](/mechanistic-views/views/subspace/)** cover the two most common background assumptions in the current literature. **[Structural](/mechanistic-views/views/structural/)** introduces the gauge-invariance argument. The **[Cases](/mechanistic-views/cases/)** section shows the views applied to real examples: IOI, induction heads, grokking, hallucination, and self-knowledge. The **[Methods](/mechanistic-views/methods/)** page classifies 15 common interpretability methods by their implicit view commitments.

The **[Formalisms](/mechanistic-views/formalism/)** section covers the mathematical objects referenced by the views: Grassmannians, gauge quotients, sheaves, holonomy, stratification. The Grassmannian page is accessible with linear algebra alone; the others benefit from graduate-level background.
